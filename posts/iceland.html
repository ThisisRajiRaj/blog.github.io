--- layout: post title: Appreciate hot showers, and other life lessons date: Sep 16, 2021 ---

I once lived in an ancient house that had no running water. The kitchen was dark, cave-like, with cold stone floors. It had a square corner with a drain set on the floor. My aunt washed vegetables, rice, plates and cups here.

At nights, this corner became a lively place. Out of nowhere, you'd hear a croak. Then more croaks, and you'd see little, slimy, brown frogs swarming out of the drain. Then came cockroaches that crawled, and then the creepiest of all, cockroaches that flew.

There were no bathrooms in the house too. To answer nature's calls, you had to draw water from a well and lug it to an "outhouse" in the backyard. In the outhouse, a single incandescent bulb dangled from the ceiling. Under its glare, you had to stay alert for snakes and scorpions. You heated water on wood stoves to wash yourselves if you wanted hot water, your clothes and hair absorbing the smell if burning wood.

I barely registered any of these as problems or "lack" back then. All I wanted, as a little girl that lived with her aunt, was warmth and love. I didn't mind that there was no easy access to bathrooms or water. I just accepted what I had.

Likewise, today I accept other things without ever noticing. In my current reality, I do not have to wait in line or draw water from a well for drinking water. My kitchen, though never spotlessly clean despite my concerted attempts, is well-lit and warm. Other than the occasional spider, I have no critter in my bathrooms. Hot water gushes out of my shower the moment I turn it on. But I do not notice them or the comforts they offer. I do not stop to appreciate or feel grateful. Except when I travel….

I often travel with a single backpack over my back. Traveling with a single backpack means you have just your bare necessities with you. You learn to live minimally. On the other hand, when you do get access to more than those bare necessities, you take notice. And taking notice is half the battle toward appreciating.

-

At the end of August, I backpacked in Iceland for two weeks, thru-hiking the famous Laugavegur trail.


Scenes from my Laugavegur hike

At the end of this 5-day adventure, I stayed in a hotel in Reykjavik where I could finally lay my head on soft, down pillows and sleep on a queen-size mattress covered in starched white sheets. I could shower in piping hot water and feel warm at the end of it. I could walk over to a restaurant and order myself a plate of spicy Pad Thai and sweet coconut milk tea. I noticed every single luxury after my deprivation, and I appreciated them more for it. This is one of the benefits of travel: you step briefly away from the things you are used to so they seem delightful to you when you return to them. Absence does make the heart grow fonder!

Travel also gives you a mental break, something I needed sorely this year. When you travel, you make new friends, try new things, get new experiences. You learn about other countries and cultures.

I learned a lot about Iceland in this trip. Firstly, that the country is breathtakingly beautiful. Secondly, that the land is as merciless as it is beautiful. The wind whips you about and the rain slashes your face. Iceland is on a hot spot where hot lava pours out of the earth, AND it straddles two tectonic plates. Just recently the country saw over 18000 earthquakes in a week and an erupting volcano. Thanks to this inhospitable habitat, much vegetation doesn't survive here natively. But the resourceful Icelandic people have found a way to thrive. They grow vegetables in greenhouses using their abundant geothermal energy. They roof their houses in corrugated metal to circumvent the lack of wood. People, like in other Scandinavian countries, are exceedingly trusting of others. They leave their children in strollers outside cafes (gasp!) and do not lock their car - we accidentally stepped into someone else's car thinking it was our rental car. Contrary to any Nordic stereotypes, the people are warm and friendly. Veganism is well understood and catered to, even though the native Icelandic cuisine has some "interesting" foods like fermented shark. Even in rural areas and fishing towns, you can find something explicitly marked vegan.



Any journey where you step out of your normal life and disconnect can widen your soul, and this was one such journey. I also learned some deeper life lessons on this vacation. Here are a few.


1. Find the truth for yourself

Reading up on other people's experiences is good (thank you for reading this far!), but know that each person perceives the world differently. What feels like climbing Mt. Everest for someone else might feel like just a moderately challenging hike for you, and vice versa. So do not let other people's worries, experiences, and fears stop you - on your adventures, in your career, in your life. Take precautions, learn the map ahead, but set out on your journey. Do not get stopped by your fears about how something is too daunting, too difficult, too beyond you.


2. Focus on one step at a time.

The terrain ahead may be tricky to navigate, but if you keep your focus, look at where you are putting your feet, and keep calm, you will get through even the most challenging obstacles. Do not let your imaginations unrelated to just the few feet in front of you drain you.


3. Use successes to fuel future successes.


If you climbed a difficult mountain or did something you were scared of before, feel proud of your accomplishment. Make a mental note and linger on what you just did. Next time you are up against something equally formidable, recall this success and use that to build up your self-confidence.


4. See things in perspective

Just before I left Iceland, I went to see a live volcano called Fagradalsfjall. The volcano had started erupting in March of this year and had been spewing sulfuric steam, ash, and molten magma ever since.

I had never seen anything like it before - the viscous magma, its slow pace, the sizzle, the rising steam, the singeing heat that got hotter as you closed in. I stared at its destruction in awe, realizing the smallness of my worries, my expectations, my disappointments, my squabbles, my pet peeves, and my self-criticism.


Volcanoes are powerful, timeless forces that shape this planet. They annihilate everything in their path, and that annihilation lays the base for more creation. In the face of such limitless, endless cycles of this universe, I realized our petty affairs are transitory. Everything shall pass one day, our most traumatizing worries, our biggest embarrassments, yur most intense crises, even our most beautiful works…


5. Understand what full life means to you and live it

In anticipation of my trip to Iceland, I binged on several Nordic-themed movies including the Amazon Prime series "Vikings". There is a point in the series, when a beloved character dies, you hear: "Weep not…I have lived a full life." What does living a full life mean to you? For me, it includes wins and losses, joys and sorrows, and LOTS and lots of rich experiences. Hiking the Laugavegur and road tripping around Iceland gave me many such wonderful experiences.

There is so much that this beautiful world offers that we can fill our lives with. At the end of my life, I hope I can look back and feel the way this beloved character felt about hers. That I have lived a full life.
