--- layout: post title: It takes more than a village date: Nov 02, 2021 ---

About three years ago, I was in a quandary. I was an Engineering Director for a service that I loved. I had been sponsoring and building that service within my organization from when it had just half a dev on it. From when it was not even a service, just a bunch of data dumped into a spreadsheet that we emailed to our customers. The team and the service had grown, and our customers were loving it.

It was at this time, due to some organizational changes too boring to get into in this blog post, I was looking for a new role. Many of you have asked me how I know it is time to look for a new role. I have told you in the past that I reflect on four things about my situation:

  • Am I learning new things in the current role?

  • Am I working with people I respect and enjoy working with?

  • Am I making a meaningful difference with the tech I am building?

  • Am I feeling good about my career prospects?

In this case, some of these things checked out and some didn't. So I started looking for something new. Thankfully, Microsoft is a place where opportunities are plentiful. I had the privilege to talk to many teams and VPs. Finally, I settled on a role in the Azure team. The opportunity in front of me was to bring a brand-new Azure service to life. Learning to build startups inside larger companies had become something of a passion for me and this fit the bill.

The service we were to build was to be used by developers and IT Professionals around the world to test quickly and efficiently. It would help them stay confident that their applications always worked. It would help them test more easily using the magic of data and machine learning, and with integration to services like Power Automate. Our first job was to lay out a plan and hire the right team to bring this service to life.

And we did. In the beginning, we called this new service Update Staging Lab. In year 1 of the service being built, we were a team of less than 10 people in Redmond, feverishly discussing what the product should be. What was the Total Addressable Market? What were the stages of building the service? What should the MVP be? Where was the funding coming from and how to pitch the service right? What was the business model? What were our operating principles? So many questions to pose and answer. These were very exciting times for me, personally, and for my team.

But even more exciting times were coming. A year in, we were working with about 20 customers on the MVP. One of our operating principles was to work with customers from day 1. We wanted to build something that people wanted - not something we built in a vacuum and had to sell later. Another principle was to be experimental, not to let analysis paralyze us, but use data to be agile in getting to market. We wrote lots of code and were scrappy about getting it live and getting feedback.

We announced Private Preview at Ignite 2019, just a year after we came together as a team. In the meantime, we had traveled to Lagos, Nigeria to hire our first international team, one of Microsoft's first teams in our Africa Development Center. In just a few months after this - in 2020 - spurred on by a pandemic, the whole world was forced to learn how to effectively work in remote settings. Our team started learning these lessons early.

As soon as we onboarded our Lagos team, we learned that we needed to set up folks with autonomy, get used to more asynchronous communication channels, relentlessly drive for clarity in what we needed to accomplish together, limit meetings where everyone needed to be in a room (physically or otherwise) at the same time, be intentional about scheduling meetings at times that will work for folks in their time zone, and so on.


For these adjustments to our working style and culture, we got plenty in return. Our Lagos team brought in new energy, new ways of reasoning over problems. We hired bright, new talent. Our product documentation got better. Our service excellence improved, thanks to having folks in different parts of the globe paying attention to Production. We learned flexibility is table stakes for doing business. We trained ourselves to be more inclusive, no matter if you are in a room with us or thousands of miles away. As a team, we were now creative and productive 'round the clock'. Most importantly, we *loved* the multi-cultural aspect of our team, celebrating cultural events together, learning things from each other.

In the summer of 2020, we announced our new name, Test Base for Microsoft 365, along with many new features. We welcomed engineers on yet another continent - this time in Shanghai - to work on Test Base.



Now we were starting to build a truly global service with our global team, spread over three continents. Onboarding new members remotely, working toward a Public Preview, and being compassionate and supportive of each other during a pandemic kept us on our toes. We reminded each other to think with both our heart and our head, to make decisions nimbly, to respond to customer needs, and yet slow down enough to take care of ourselves.

This July, we announced Public Preview and made the service accessible to anyone who wants to preview it. Since then, over 500 companies and organizations have taken advantage of the service, doubling down on their testing and quality. Some of these customers can now start testing their applications weeks before Windows Updates come out, mere days after a build becomes available in the Windows engineering lab. Something that has never been available before.

We are now entering a new chapter for Test Base. At Ignite this morning, we annouced General Availability for Test Base. Check it out here. We are enabling more features and workflows for our commercial customers. Test Base now supports testing applications against Windows 11 builds. There is so much more in the offering that Rama goes into depth on, so be sure to read about it all in his blog.

I am incredibly proud of it all, proud of what we have accomplished, and how we have weathered storms together. I am grateful for my team and all the leaders that have leaned in to make this happen. It's been about three years since Test Base was just an idea on a word document on our SharePoint, two years since we became a global team. As I look back, these have been eventful years that have taught me a lot.

It indeed takes more than an isolated village to build a truly global, world-class service. Our diversity is our strength. Having folks build Test Base from different parts of the world makes the service more suitable for a global market and a global audience. It makes it easier to tend to our customers' needs quickly. I am glad to see more companies adapt to a more remote and hybrid workplace, where our colleagues can be anywhere in the world. I truly think this makes us a better team. But we still have work to do. We need to continue to deepen our learning to support - not just allow - remote workers. We need to adapt our workplace culture and mindset to be more flexible, inclusive, and open. Test Base is a service built to support an important function - testing - to be done from anywhere. We need to use more services like it to change the way we work together - to work from anywhere in the world. That is how we can thrive in this new era.
