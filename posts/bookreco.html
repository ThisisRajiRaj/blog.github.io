--- layout: post title: 20 books from 20+ years of reading date: June 08, 2024 ---

I was not an early reader. When I was little, life was difficult, money was scarce, and there was not a lot of opportunity for recreational book reading.

After immigrating to Canada, I found myself suddenly in a world of abundance – I still remember my disbelief mixed with glee when I first went to a public library. I started reading ravenously. It was almost as though I was making up for my first two decades of not being a reader. I would read when having coffee, read at lunch, read in the evenings when others in the house watched TV, read in bed, even read on my way to college. I confess I continue to do most of that even today.

Reading is still my favorite pastime, a steady companion, something that has seen me through boredom and loneliness and turmoil and grief. It has helped me grow as a human, taught me to be philosophical, honed my leadership skills, shined light on human nature, made me marvel at the magic of the world, helped me take care of myself. These days I read books but also listen to them when I am cooking or when I am out running. There are so many ways to open your mind and learn through reading, so if you are not reading a book or two every few months, I urge you to give this fabulous habit a try. You might fall in love just as I did.



Here are 20 book recommendations from my more than 20 years of reading.

1. A Room of One’s Own : A feminist classic with beautiful writing, this book’s premise is that one needs space (physical and mental) and stability to find her own voice. I read this for the first time when I was in my early 20s. It was empowering, a call to claim my space in the world and share my voice.

2. The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People : Unlike preachy self-help books, Covey’s thoughtful approach encourages prioritizing the important and not getting drowned in the urgent, defining what success means to you, and seeking to understand others before trying to be understood. It’s packed with valuable leadership and personal lessons.

3. When Breath Becomes Air : I don’t often reread books – there are so many books out there that I find it hard to justify going back to something I have already read. This one is an exception. I read this book a few years ago, and I was moved by the poignancy of the writing. It’s a deeply touching memoir of Dr. Paul Kalanidhi, a neurosurgeon who was diagnosed with terminal lung cancer. He explores the meaning of life as he his own death draws nearer. Strange as it might sound, rereading this after my dad passed away gave me much solace and appreciation for the impermanence of life.

4. Drive: The Surprising Truth about What Motivates Us : I was training in Computer Science through college. When I became a developer, I could immediately put my training to use. But when I became a manager, no part of my schooling helped me with understanding human motivation. This is where books like Drive come in. In this book, Pink’s thesis is that humans are not just motivated by external rewards like money. Some of the powerful things that drive us are intrinsic, such as autonomy, mastery of a craft, and meaning in work. Over a dozen years of people management now, I’ve used these insights to help my teams thrive.

5. The Elements of Style : Every writer should read this. Everyone that needs to write – and that is EVERYONE – should read this. “Use strong, concrete words to convey your ideas effectively”, “Omit needless words”, “Avoid complex negations in a sentence” are all great advice that apply now as in 1919 when this book was published.

6. Miles from Nowhere : I was not going to do the monthlong bicycle trip along the Pacific coast from Vancouver to California with my husband. I was too nervous. I imagined all the possible ways it could go wrong. One day as we were still arguing about whether to go or not, my husband cleverly left a copy of this book on our dining table, and I picked it up to read. It was a captivating tale. In the book, the author goes on a 23,000 KM global journey on her bicycle, visiting 25 countries. I loved the stories and was mesmerized by the adventure. I changed my mind on the trip – spoiler alert: our Pacific coast bicycle trip was one of the most cherished memories of my life. Read it if you would like to see for yourself what it feels like to answer the call of slow travel.

7. Outlive: The Science and Art of Longevity : Dr. Peter Attia’s analytical prowess shines in his book, with every claim backed by scientific reasoning. I read this book while mourning my father’s death in 2024, a life claimed by one of the ‘four horsemen’ (heart disease, cancer, type 2 diabetes, and neurodegenerative disease) that Dr. Attia warns us about. Despite his seemingly extreme ideas, the book is a treasure trove of insights for enhancing life quality in old age.

8. Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind : My husband jokes that Yuval Noah Harari is one of the two author crushes I have - I drop his name in my conversations so much. This book blew my mind. History is drab for most people, and the history of all of humankind can strike you as a boring read. But the way Harari spins this yarn, he has you turning the pages. Here is a thought-provoking concept for you from the book: most things we consider real and base our daily lives on – money, religion, government, laws – are not real. They exist because humans conjured them as ideas and these ideas stuck with a large group of people. I can’t think of money the same way ever again.

9. Thinking Fast and Slow : Continuing with my author crushes, Daniel Kahneman. Kahneman is a brilliant behavioral scientist who made me (and millions of others) aware of the quirks and biases in our one head. He talks about the fast “System 1” in your brain that you often use to make snap decisions and the more deliberate “System 2” that you need to use for complex tasks and problems. System 1 being fast is great, but can also land us in trouble because it gets trapped in illusions and biases. This book is a bible for anyone who wants to understand how their own mind – or another human’s – works.

10. The God Delusion : There was a time in my life when, thanks to being brought up in an orthodox family, I was super religious. It is because of scientists like Richard Dawkins that I began to question my own superstitions and delusions. Dawkins is most certainly a controversial figure, someone who virulently criticizes organized religion. But if you can put aside his rhetoric and just try to learn from his arguments, this would is a thought-provoking read.

11. The Untethered Soul : This is a strange recommendation to follow the one about “delusion”. In this book, Michael Singer talks about some things that are most definitely outlandish and strain your logical mind. But there is one concept that has really helped me and that is the only reason this book in in my list: what we understand as ‘self’ – our constant stream of thoughts and emotions – is neither a constant nor our true self. It is our consciousness that keeps us alive that remains constant (even though that too is a ’controlled hallucination’ of our minds ). The more often you pull yourself back into that consciousness rather than operating in the realm of thought and emotions, the more peace and balance you’ll find.

12. My Life in Full : This book is not only a beautiful read, but inspiring too. Indra Nooyi is a legend, a great leader, and a wonderful writer. This book chronicles her journey from her childhood in India, her education at Yale, to her rise as one of the world's most powerful businesswomen. Nooyi shares her insights on leadership, career growth, work-life balance, and the role of diversity in the workforce.

13. Big Magic : Elizabeth Gilbert writes splendidly, but I have to confess: I always have a love-hate relationship with her books. I gave up and went back to Eat, Pray, Love at least a handful of times. Her woo-woo turns me away, but her style and lyrical writing bring me back to her books. Big Magic was mostly in the “love” territory for me. Here is one of my highlights in the book: “We all spend our twenties and thirties trying so hard to be perfect, because we’re so worried about what people will think of us. Then we get into our forties and fifties, and we finally start to be free, because we decide that we don’t give a damn what anyone thinks of us. But you won’t be completely free until you reach your sixties and seventies, when you finally realize this liberating truth—nobody was ever thinking about you, anyhow.”

14. Becoming : Becoming is Michelle Obama’s memoir. I loved the intimacy, honesty, the relatability and sheer beauty of her writing. She says, “I belonged at Princeton, as much as anybody. And I came from the South Side of Chicago. It felt important to say out loud.” As someone that comes from a no-name town in the South of India, I can totally understand this feeling of wanting to say to every minority listening that we belong no matter whatever stories are, no matter where we come from. Hats off to Obama for saying this so eloquently.

15. Walden : This book is a ponderous read, but full of brilliant ideas. Thoreau critiques our tendency to materialism and calls on us to connect with nature more. If you think this is just some hermetic weirdo’s idea, think again. There is a lot of scientific evidence to the benefits of being in the woods/nature. Reading this not only helps us explore non-materialism but also kindles a desire to be outdoors more.

16. Four Thousand Weeks: Time Management for Mortals : Embracing your limits – now that’s a foreign concept for many of us. But Oliver Burkeman argues that there is no other way to live your life. We are limited in many ways – what our bodies can do, how many days we have on earth, what can truly remain after we are gone. Paradoxically the way to a tranquil and productive life is to accept these limits and focus on doing the next right thing. I love this book so much I wrote a blog post about it .

17. Reading Lolita in Tehran : At the heart of this book is Nafisi’s secret book club, which she hosted during her last two years in Iran. The club, consisting of a handpicked group of female former students, met weekly at Nafisi’s house to discuss forbidden Western classics. They read and discussed works of English literature, with particular importance placed on the works of Vladimir Nabokov, F. Scott Fitzgerald, Henry James, and Jane Austen. The book is a reminder of the power of books to empower and inspire.

18. Life of Pi : What makes this novel stand out in my memory is its beautiful, philosophical ending. I won’t give away any spoilers, but just saw that it is spectacular and unique tale through and through.

19. A Game of Thrones : Don’t judge me, but I am OBSESSED with this series. I watch it on HBO repeatedly and never get bored. Teading these books was a sheer joy. Don’t let their length deter you. The book series brims with political intrigue, medieval imagery, battles, strong women leaders, magic, passion, betrayal and so much more. And then, of course, there are dragons –how can you say no to that! 😊

20. The Pillars of the Earth : If I remember right, The Pillars was my introduction to historical fiction, and this is a genre that has captivated me now. The book is meticulously researched and detailed, and yet a delightful page turner. It is set in medieval England and explores commerce, religion, and politics of the era through its most memorable characters.

Man’s search for meaning : Ok, I said 20 recommendations, but I can’t end the list without this one. This was the last book that my father gifted me and I will cherish it for just that fact. But this book is more than that. Here are couple of profound quotes from Frankl to end this post with.

“Forces beyond your control can take away everything you possess except one thing, your freedom to choose how you will respond to the situation. You cannot control what happens to you in life, but you can always control what you will feel and do about what happens to you.”

“Don’t aim at success—the more you aim at it and make it a target, the more you are going to miss it. For success, like happiness, cannot be pursued; it must ensue, and it only does so as the unintended side-effect of one’s dedication to a cause greater than oneself.”
