I recently watched "The Darkest Hour", a movie about some crucial events of World War II. In it, there is a moment when Churchill's political career AND the fate of the allied countries seem poised on a knife's edge. The members of the British parliament have lost faith in his direction. At that exact moment, Churchill rises from his seat and makes a rousing speech. Like the viewers of the movie, his colleagues are moved and energized. They pledge their support to him. The army is mobilized, and the rest is history. At the end of that scene, one of Churchill's colleagues remarks that Churchill had just "mobilized the English language and sent it into battle".

Here is a less well-known fact about Churchill, the man who was famous for his words and speeches. In his first ever speech to the parliament, he froze for a full three minutes, then mutterered a few words before sitting down. Many of us similarly struggle with speaking in public. Speaking well can help you have greater impact on the world. It can help you persuade others to make things happen. It can help your career. So how can one become a better public speaker?

After my earlier blog post on Thinking Traps, a few folks reached out to me with that question. I promised that I'd write up what I had learned myself over the years and share.


There are two types of speakers: those who get nervous and those who are liars.

-- Mark Twain


Public speaking doesn't come naturally to me. When I started in this industry, I had six years of training in computer science but little understanding of how to communicate well. For the first few years, my work had been all about my code. I was expected to collaborate with others but speaking to groups of people was not expected. Very soon though - and much sooner than one might expect - expectations started to shift. As a senior engineer and then as a manager, communicating clearly and confidently became important. Then, I started presenting to customers and to the leaders of my company.

I have had the good fortune of working at a company that invests in its employees. Microsoft gave me a ton of resources - 1:1 presentation coaching, useful feedback from managers, free LinkedIn Learning courses - that I used to learn the art of public speaking. So here is what I learned.


Let's start with the "what".

Openings are important - not just for your speech, but for any content. Whether it is an email to your team or a novel you read or a movie you watch, openings can make or break the deal. Try to capture the attention of your audience with your opening. With so many flashy things that vie for everyone's attention these days, getting your audience quickly interested is crucial.

Tell a story. A lot of what we present in our day jobs can feel like disparate sets of facts and figures. But there is always a human story to tell. There is always a narrative, a story arc, that you can weave. It could be from the perspective of a single customer. It could be a grand end vision you are painting for your area. Find the right story arc, especially one that speaks in the currency of your listener's heart.

If you are using slides, do not cram them with words. It is almost impossible for people to pay attention to multiple things simultaneously. If your slides have too many words, your audience is spending their attention on parsing them and not on listening to you. A single picture is not just worth a thousand words, it can also anchor your audience's attention.

The biggest favor you can do to yourself, and your audience, is to slowwwww the heck down. When I was given this feedback myself, many times, I would think that I had slowed down only to realize that I had not *actually* slowed down, or I had not slowed down enough. The time it takes in a group setting for your audience to follow you tends to be way more than you realize. Slowing down and pausing between words helps your listeners cognitively catch up with you.

Slowing down also helps you. You tend to speak faster when you are nervous, but speaking fast makes you breathe shallower, and consequently more nervous. This endless vicious cycle can be interrupted if you just pause and take breaths. Doing so brought the biggest improvement by far in my public speaking. I ended up coming across more confident and, because I was not increasing my anxiety with ragged breaths, I was able to think more calmly as well.

Use short sentences to further reduce the cognitive strain for your audience. Additionally, do not run on with your points. We tend to be subject matter experts in what we are speaking and therefore we suffer from what is called the "knowledge curse". Knowledge curse makes it difficult for experts to explain things clearly and concisely to people they communicate with. It makes you unfocused - your head crammed with so many things you want to convey at once - and you start rambling. Make your point, succinctly, and move on to the next point you want to make.

Empathize and connect with your audience. Know what your audience's background is beforehand and speak to their heart as well as their brains. Understand what they are looking for and what makes their world go around. Make eye contact. Talk to a single person in the front row and sustain your gaze for a couple of seconds before moving to the next person in the audience.

Calm your pre-talk jitters with deep breathing and affirmations that work for you. For example, you could say to yourself, "These are my people. They are rooting for me to do well". Instead of saying "I am nervous", tell yourself that you feel excited to be there.

Lastly, be authentic. The worst you can do is try to be somebody else. Perhaps you admire someone who is naturally funny and jokes in all their talks. Perhaps you want your audience to laugh as well. But don't try to add in jokes if that is not your style. One of my friends once told me that she feels like she is listening to a professor when she in one of my talks. Whereas her own speaking style makes me feel more like I am listening to a no-nonsense older sister. I adore my friend's style, but it's not for me. Instead of trying to be her, if I tried to be the best possible me, I would end up being more authentic for my audience.

In summary:

  1. Slow down. Pause more than you think you should.
  2. Breathe. Take mindful breaths before you begin. Take more mindful breaths when you pause.
  3. Find the story in what you want to say and stick to that story arc. Do not be verbose in your speech or in your slides.
  4. Connect with your audience. Understand the currency of their heart.
  5. Be true to yourself and your style.
  6. Smile. Calm your nerves using affirmations and by giving yourself the permission to be imperfect.

After all these years, it is still an effort for me to get up on stage and talk. It still makes me uncomfortable and anxious. I make mistakes and struggle to follow the advice above. But all of that is ok. Many good leaders I know experience the same. None of them are perfect all the time. Perfection, in my view, is an elusive and a needless goal in many areas of life. Show yourself the compassion to challenge the tyranny of perfection. Good luck!
