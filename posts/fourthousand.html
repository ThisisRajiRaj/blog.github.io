--- layout: post title: Four thousand weeks: a case for embracing what you can't control date: February 09, 2022 ---

When my mother died one fine morning thirty-nine years ago, we didn't expect it. My father was just thirty-seven at the time. I am sure that at that moment he was devastated, shaken up. If he had considered his future stretching out in front of him, I am guessing he would have seen uncertainty. He might have imagined a bleak picture. He might have been worried about how he was going to raise two young children, all on his own from then on.

Fast forward almost four decades, and our family is just fine. Sure, we endured challenges and setbacks, just like many people and many families do around the globe. But we still love our dad, and we are all still mostly ok. My mother passing away, while heart-rending, didn't mean a catastrophe for us. We dealt with it as we always deal with any obstacle in front of us. Life unfolded along a dimension that I am sure my dad had never imagined before.

But that's how future is - not fully in our control and often surprising us. Yet at moments of uncertainty, we get all worked up because we want future to be entirely in our control. We want future to materialize exactly as we envision it. We want it to not surprise us.


Our efforts to influence the future aren't the problem. The problem-the source of all the anxiety-is the need that we feel, from our vantage point here in the present moment, to be able to know that those efforts will prove successful.

- Oliver Burkeman


But trying to control everything about the future is a fool's errand. We can have a strong desire for certain outcomes, for sure. We can craft a strong plan to help us achieve those outcomes. Our control over those outcomes, however, is limited. It is best for our mental health to internalize that fact fully. That is one of the core ideas behind the book "Four Thousand Weeks: Time Management for Mortals" by Oliver Burkeman" .

I read a lot of books - books that help me with my work, trashy novels that are just entertainment, and everything in between. Every once in a while, I read a book that offers me a different way to look at life. Four Thousand Weeks was one such book.



Have you ever pondered what you want from your life? Many of us think that we want to be successful. Society often defines success on the surface: how productive you are, how wealthy, how big is your title, if your personal life looks like a perfect Regency dance, and so on.

But where does that all lead to?

We have a limited amount of time on earth, about four thousand weeks on average human lifespan. We have limited control over the eternal future of this universe or even the more constrained future of our lifetime. We have limited abilities at the many things we can possibly do with our time here. We can only do a limited number of things at any moment and, cumulatively, in our life. Given all that, the author Oliver Burkeman argues, our best course of action is to fully embrace our limits and still carry on with what we must do.


Perhaps most radically of all, seeing and accepting our limited powers over our time can prompt us to question the very idea that time is something you use in the first place. There is an alternative: the unfashionable but powerful notion of letting time use you, approaching life not as an opportunity to implement your predetermined plans for success but as a matter of responding to the needs of your place and your moment in history.

- Oliver Burkeman


Embracing these limits, he proffers, is strangely liberating. When you are not overwhelmed about all the things that you could be doing at any moment, you are fully present for what you're doing and you enjoy it all the more. When you are not worried about how other people in an unknowable future will react, you are free to do your work authentically and bring forth value for the present. When you are not anxious about outcomes, about the uncertain future, you just do the work that needs to be done.

We are always told to NOT accept our limits. Not accepting our limits is at the heart of Growth Mindset. I am a big fan of learning constantly, and Growth Mindset wonderfully appeals to me. I could become a runner only because I didn't limit myself when was in my twenties that I could not become an athlete after being inactive all those years. I could continue to write only because I believed I could continue to improve my writing.

But Burkeman's limit-embracing view had a role to play in it too. I knew I would never become a world-class sprinter. That was not a goal I could manage along with the other pursuits in my life. I didn't have a genetic advantage in the sport. I was ok accepting these limits. I knew I would really well in some areas of my life, and in other areas, it was just ok to be average.

This is not a view we often embrace consciously. And Four Thousand Weeks asks us do exactly that. We are lucky to be alive. We had little control over most of the things that made us - who our parents were, how they raised us etc. - and yet we are here, and most of us, most of the time, we are just fine. Lack of control does not mean a life you cannot manage. Burkeman drives this point in a dozen excellent chapters.


Once you give up on the unattainable goal of eradicating all your problems, it becomes possible to develop an appreciation for the fact that life just is a process of engaging with problem after problem, giving each one the time it requires-that the presence of problems in your life, in other words, isn't an impediment to a meaningful existence but the very substance of one.

- Oliver Burkeman


Whether you are overwhelmed about the innumerable things you can't get to, or you feel you are not being productive enough, or pondering your life, legacy, and the future, or simply want to get acquainted with a different philosophy of life, I recommend this book to you. It might just change your life, or at least how at peace you are with it.
