--- layout: post title: What I talked about when I was asked to talk at the UN about empowering women through tech date: March 19, 2023 ---

Do you have someone in your life that is so kind that you feel like you're in the presence of pure goodness?

I have a few such folks in my life, but the one that stuns me every time with her kindness is my friend Jane.

Jane is my running friend. I have run with her for over 10 years now, and she is very dear to me. Recently, Jane volunteered to accompany me on my trip to New York. It was the first time I was going to speak at the United Nations on a panel on empowering women and girls through tech. Jane, the kindest soul I know, wanted to be there with me and FOR me.

The first time I had heard about the UN was when I was a wide-eyed girl in a small town in India. I had an arthritic grip of the English language. The Egyptian statesman Boutros Boutros-Ghali was the UN's Secretary General. I had just learned about the formation of the UN and how it was an organization dedicated to world peace and security. I was awed. I didn’t imagine, as a high schooler then, that I’d even visit the UN one day, let alone be a speaker there. So, when I was invited to speak – not just speak, but speak on the topic of empowering girls and women through digital education – I was overjoyed.

The day of my UN talk dawned grey and cold. As we headed out of our hotel, a biting wind cut into my skin, feeling like a million needles. There was a persistent rain mixed with drifting soft snowflakes. In Seattle, I usually carry a rain jacket with me on such days. In NY, because I was dressed in fine clothes, I took an umbrella with me.



On our way to the UN, we ducked into a takeaway joint close by and - struck by the tenor of the activity inside – we picked up a quick breakfast and a cup of coffee to go. Struggling with the umbrella on my one gloved hand and the coffee cup on the other, trying simultaneously to fiddle with my phone to get more information on the exact location of my session, we scurried over to the official headquarters of the United Nations.

To be in that building, with so many people from around the world, warmed my heart. This world is beautiful in its diversity. My panel was a wonderful selection of "Power Women" - all execs in technology. We had Kranthi Darapu, a General Manager at Amazon; Ali Heron, the CTO of Petal; Anna Kowalczuk, a VP at MongoDB. Kate Watts, the moderator of my panel asked us all many questions that day. But the one that rounded up our session sums it up for me: what advice did I have for the policy makers gathered in that room that day to get more women into tech?

When I grew up, as I describe in my book “Daring to be Different”, I faced many gender-based prejudices. I could only eat after all the men in the house had been fed. I had to clean up after my brother. When I got older, my place in the world was supposed to be that of a wife, a mother, a daughter-in-law. I was supposed to not argue, to be pleasing and quiet. Despite all that, I was never told I would not be good at Math and Science. When I came to North America, and I heard that girls growing up here routinely pick up that societal message, I realized that different cultures struggle with inequalities in different ways.


Inequalities may be a shared experience across cultures, but each culture struggles with inequality in its own unique way.


Policies that will work to bring more women into tech (and help them thrive there) in Kenya may not be the same as what will be needed in India or USA or Iran. We need to sift for our shared experiences and recommend policies to address those.Bbut one size seldom fits all when it comes to heterogeneity. Across the board, it is important for us to build flexibility into work, assist with family care, to build recruiting pipelines for diverse individuals, to foster an inclusive environment, and create opportunities for professional development. But we should also not lose sight of the fact that the nature of challenges are different in different places. Our response to global challenges should be nuanced. We need to build uniquely local solutions for uniquely local problems. This is why it is important to have local champions and leaders and make sure their voice is heard.

It was truly an honor for me to be at the UN sharing my perspectives on a topic that is so close to my heart.

I was in NYC for my UN event. But the universe has a way of surprising you with serendipity. It so happened that, that week, the NY chapter of the Women at Microsoft Employee Resource Group had been invited to ring the closing bell at NASDAQ! I had presented a talk to this group at lunchtime and the women invited me to join them at NASDAQ. Of course, I said yes.

I love New York. It always galvanizes me and gets me to do more. The key is to be open to these new experiences that come your way. From hanging out with my lovely friend Jane to speaking at the UN to the talk I gave at Microsoft to the NASDAQ closing bell to getting to catch up with some family and friends, NY gave me magical few days last week. I am always to waiting to return to one of my favorite cities in the world.
